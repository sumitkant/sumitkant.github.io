Section 01 · Focus on essentials

Filtering the Essentials

Start here — in order

  • 1Identify the main process — what should the audience take away?
  • 2Break it into a logical sequence of limited, clear steps.
  • 3Ask who's involved — stakeholders clarify the flow of actions.
  • 4Know your audience: replace jargon with plain language.

Quality bar

  • Keep it simple, keep it clear — essentials only.
  • Accuracy is crucial: verify with a reputable source, get expert sign-off.
  • Tell a story — guide the audience through a narrative.
  • Contextualize data so its significance is understood.

4 limitations — and what to do instead

1Information overloadToo much detail leaves viewers overwhelmed and unsure of the key takeaways.
InsteadFocus on the essentials needed to understand the overall process.
2OversimplificationBeing too concise leaves viewers with an incomplete understanding.
InsteadFocus on a specific aspect of the larger issue.
3MisrepresentationA misleading chart or icon distorts the message — e.g. pie slices that don't total 100%.
InsteadEnsure every visual is clear and accurate.
4Complex, static dataInfographics can't dynamically explore intricate relationships.
InsteadPair them with videos or interactive presentations.

Design Principles

The 7 elements — your building blocks

Space
Balance & breathing room — negative space is your friend
Line
Guides the eye, creates borders, adds emphasis
Shape
Triangle = direction & action · Circle = community & unity
Size
Bigger = more important — builds the hierarchy of attention
Color
Evokes emotion, mood & brand identity
Value
Light ↔ dark creates depth and contrast
Typography
Style & size drive readability and appeal
All 7 together
= balance, contrast & harmony

Visual hierarchy — what the eye meets first

  • 1Bold heading first — say exactly what this is.
  • 2Subtitle smaller; key steps get larger icons & text.
  • 3Placement is crucial: first key point top-left, where reading starts.
  • 4Contrasting colors highlight the key steps.

Clear layout

  • Align everything on an invisible grid.
  • Don't clutter — leave room for each step to breathe.
  • Arrows / numbered icons guide the reader top-to-bottom, the way we read.
  • Keep secondary shapes small — never the main focus.
"A well designed infographic is a bridge, not a maze."Design connects your content to a captivated, well-prepared audience.

Section 03 · Emotion · contrast · consistency

Color Theory

Choosing colors

  • Colors evoke emotion — use them strategically to guide the viewer.
  • Build a palette of 5–6 colors; a scheme generator helps when in a hurry. Save it for reuse.
  • Use universally recognized meanings — red = warning — for key steps.
  • Value (light ↔ dark) adds depth and contrast.

Applying colors

  • Balance bold accents with a calming neutral background.
  • Contrast text vs. background for readability in any lighting.
  • Brightest color on the heading (seen first); secondary shapes stay subtle.
  • Stick to your palette throughout — it ties the design together.

Section 04 · Readability first · one family

Typography

Rules

  • Readability is the #1 consideration — the right font can make or break the infographic.
  • Clear, bold fonts legible on screen, in print, and from a distance.
  • Sans serif = clean, legible, modern — great on screens.
  • A display face may headline; body fonts must not clash with it.
  • Reuse the heading font at smaller sizes for design consistency.

Section 05 · The crucial step before publishing

Taking Feedback

Ask 3 kinds of reviewers

Subject-matter experts
Confirm accuracy & alignment with existing protocols
Colleagues across departments
Diverse perspectives surface confusion
Someone unfamiliar
The true first-time test of your content

Ask specific questions — not "what do you think?"

  • Clarity — are steps easy to follow, even under stress?
  • Visual flow — does it guide you logically? Are images clear?
  • Prioritization — essentials only, no overwhelming detail?

Evaluate & act — in order

  • 1Recurring themes flagged by multiple reviewers are the most crucial to fix.
  • 2Content revisions — add missing prerequisites, clarify distinctions.
  • 3Visual tweaks — bigger arrows for flow, brighter background for visibility.
  • 4Clarity boost — simplify jargon, swap text for universal symbols. Then polish, get approval, share.
"Feedback is your friend."Test whether your content is not only accurate, but understandable.

Section 06 · Habits · working with AI · 6 types

What Makes Infographics Great

"If a picture is worth 1,000 words, then an infographic is like a whole library condensed into a single image."
The reason every principle on this page matters — density with clarity.

Core habits

  • Break information into digestible chunks — brains respond to visuals and remember them.
  • Step-by-step, like a recipe — pictures, not merely words.
  • Aim for a picture dictionary: understandable in any language, easy to share, a quick reference that empowers the viewer.

Working with AI

  • Human judgment ensures accuracy, avoids bias, and tailors the result to context and audience.
  • Give as much context as possible — the clearer the prompt, the better the content.
  • Not satisfied? Rewrite the prompt until you are. The more you use AI, the better you get.

Pick the right type — match the format to the shape of your information

Statistical
Charts and graphs showcasing data and trends. Numbers lead — bar, pie and line charts carry the message, with oversized figures for the headline metric.
Best forSurvey results, performance figures, anything where the numbers are the point.
Informational
Visuals and text explaining concepts or processes. Text-heavy but visually structured — descriptive headings break a topic into ordered, readable sections.
Best forIntroducing a new topic, theme or product where there's little data to plot.
Timeline
Important events in chronological order. A single track with milestone markers emphasizes a timeframe and shows how something developed.
Best forCompany history, project roadmaps, the evolution of an idea or product.
Process
Step-by-step instructions. Connected stages with arrows break a complex workflow into simpler chunks and make the order impossible to misread.
Best forHow-to guides, onboarding, procedures people follow while working.
Geographic
Maps to represent data. The map is the main visual — heat maps show density, map series compare regions or show change over time.
Best forLocation-specific data, demographics, regional comparisons.
Comparison
Pros and cons, or features side by side. Two mirrored panels highlight similarities and differences so the audience can decide for themselves.
Best forProduct or plan comparisons, before-and-after, trade-off decisions.
