# Article Templates

## Longform reported feature

Use this for company/category stories of 1,500-2,800 words.

### 1. Headline
Specific, thesis-bearing, slightly wry.

### 2. Opening scene or contradiction
3-6 short paragraphs. Start with a person, place, quote, operational detail, or counterintuitive fact. Do not begin with broad market background.

### 3. The setup
Explain the visible story: funding, IPO, reform, expansion, partnership, regulatory event, or leadership move.

### 4. Nut graf
State the actual story underneath the visible story.

Template:
`But [company/event] is not really about [surface explanation]. It is about [business mechanism]. And that matters because [stakes now].`

### 5. First section: why the obvious story is incomplete
Show the conventional wisdom and why it fails.

### 6. Second section: the business machine
Explain unit economics, distribution, regulation, supply chain, data, credit, market structure, incentives, or governance.

### 7. Third section: pressure points
Introduce opposing forces: incumbents, cash burn, regulation, consumers, employees, banks, vendors, states, or public investors.

### 8. Fourth section: what happens next
Explain why the next phase will be harder than the story suggests.

### 9. Ending
Return to the opening contradiction and sharpen it.

## Short Nutgraf-style commentary

Use this for 700-1,200 word analytical essays or newsletter-style pieces.

### Voice
More conversational. First person is allowed, but do not make the article about the writer. The voice should guide the reader through the argument.

### Structure
1. quick personal or topical opening
2. two examples or one news peg
3. lazy take to discard
4. real mechanism
5. implications
6. crisp ending

Useful moves:
- `There are many ways to look at this.`
- `The laziest take is...`
- `That is not wrong. It is just incomplete.`
- `The more interesting question is...`

## Rewrite mode

When rewriting a user draft:
1. preserve the user's facts and claims unless clearly unsupported
2. strengthen the angle and order before changing sentences
3. compress generic background
4. add missing business mechanics as placeholders if necessary
5. keep user-provided quotes intact unless asked to polish them
6. return only the rewritten article unless the user asks for notes

## From topic-only mode

When the user provides only a topic:
1. ask whether web research is allowed
2. if research is allowed, gather current facts from reliable sources
3. if research is not allowed, produce a clearly marked speculative outline or draft framework
4. do not invent current numbers or quotes

## Drafting checklist

Before finalizing, verify:
- Is the headline a thesis, not a label?
- Does the opening contain a concrete moment or contradiction?
- Is the real story clear by the nut graf?
- Does each number have a reason to exist?
- Are incentives named explicitly?
- Is there at least one caveat or counter-pressure?
- Does the ending land on consequence rather than summary?
