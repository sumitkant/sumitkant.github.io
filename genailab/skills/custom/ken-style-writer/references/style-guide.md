# Style Guide

## Editorial posture

Write as a skeptical but fair business reporter. The voice is controlled, observant, and lightly amused by corporate ambition. The article should not sound angry, moralistic, or promotional. It should sound like someone who has read the filings, spoken to people, noticed the contradiction, and is now explaining the business machine underneath the story.

The default attitude:
- curious about incentives
- suspicious of easy narratives
- interested in unit economics and distribution power
- alert to regulatory and operational frictions
- comfortable using irony, but sparingly

## Core move: contradiction first

Most pieces begin with a contradiction:
- a company is growing but losing money
- a legacy asset is suddenly useful
- a company avoided the obvious product and won
- a reform solves the visible problem but not the structural one
- a platform claims efficiency while centralising power
- a startup sells wealth creation while demanding punishing trade-offs

Turn the contradiction into the article's operating question.

Weak: `Company X is expanding into Y.`
Strong: `Company X says Y is its next growth engine. The trouble is that Y is also the reason its old model is under pressure.`

## Opening patterns

Use one of these openings:

1. **Scene-led reported opening**
   Start with a specific place, person, date, operational detail, or moment. Then widen to the business thesis.

2. **Quote-led opening**
   Use a revealing quote, not a generic corporate quote. Follow it with why the quote matters or what it hides.

3. **Contradiction-led opening**
   Start with the obvious narrative, then undercut it.

4. **Conversational commentary opening**
   For short Nutgraf-style pieces, use first-person sparingly: `I guess`, `Here are two stories`, `The laziest take is...`, `There are many ways to look at this.` Use this only when the requested piece is commentary, not a reported feature.

## Paragraph rhythm

Use short-to-medium paragraphs. Many paragraphs should be 1-3 sentences. Insert occasional one-line paragraphs for emphasis.

Examples of acceptable one-line turns:
- `That was the easy part.`
- `The catch?`
- `And yet, it survives.`
- `This is not a coincidence.`
- `Which is where the story gets interesting.`

Do not overuse these. Two to five per longform piece is enough.

## Sentence style

Prefer plain English with precise business terms. Use Indian business vocabulary naturally: kiranas, crore, lakh, FY24, DRHP, capex, opex, Arpu, Ebitda, unit economics, distribution, CAC, GMV, private labels, credit period, working capital, regulatory dues.

Use mild wit through structure rather than jokes. The humor often comes from juxtaposition:
- `On paper, the idea is sound. The catch is that no one knows how to make it happen.`
- `The numbers got the memo.`
- `That theory did not age well.`

Avoid meme language, excessive sarcasm, and punchlines that make the story feel unserious.

## Nut graf

By the early part of the article, state the real story. A good nut graf has three parts:
1. the visible event
2. the hidden business mechanism
3. why the mechanism matters now

Pattern:
`But [visible event] is only the surface. The deeper problem/opportunity is [mechanism]. That matters because [stakes].`

## Evidence texture

Blend:
- reported quotes
- financial figures
- market estimates
- operational details
- industry context
- company response
- skeptical interpretation

Do not stack numbers without interpretation. Every number must answer: why does this change the story?

## Sources and attribution

Use attribution that preserves reporting texture:
- `said a former executive`
- `according to two people familiar with the matter`
- `per its draft IPO documents`
- `The Ken has learnt` only when the user explicitly instructs that the draft is for a fictional or internal style exercise; otherwise use `the reporting indicates` or direct attribution.

Never invent anonymous sources. If the source base lacks interviews, write around documents and public evidence.

## Headline style

Headlines should be specific, slightly opinionated, and built around a tension. They often use:
- contrast: `X did A. Now B is catching up.`
- metaphor: `the party is sobering up`, `kitchen math`, `afterlife`
- accumulation: `the runway, the lounge, the latte, and the lipstick`
- sharp claim: `No one knows how X works. Not even Y.`

Avoid clickbait that overpromises. The headline should already contain the thesis.

## Section headings

Use short headings with a turn of phrase. They should not be generic labels like `Background` or `Market Overview`.

Good patterns:
- `Gone flat`
- `The usual update`
- `The autonomy of investment`
- `Goodbye, aggregators. Hello, gatekeepers`
- `A degree in doubt`

## Endings

End by reframing the central contradiction. Do not conclude with bland advice. The ending should leave the reader with the business consequence.

Good ending shapes:
- the company can survive, but only by becoming something different
- the reform fixes the visible leak but not the institution behind it
- the moat was hiding in what everyone dismissed as baggage
- the growth story depends on a trade-off nobody wants to say aloud

## Do-not-write list

Avoid:
- `In today's fast-paced world`
- `game-changer`
- `disruptive innovation` unless quoted or critiqued
- `only time will tell`
- generic SWOT language
- breathless praise
- fake certainty
- invented sourcing
- long academic paragraphs
