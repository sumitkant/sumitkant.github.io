# Category Playbooks

## Technology and AI

Default lens: incentives, cost, adoption, power shifts, and workflow reality. Avoid AI hype. Show the gap between demo and economics.

Common angles:
- free or subsidised usage turns into cost discipline
- AI adoption creates new internal incentives and budget problems
- distribution deals look like user acquisition but may be about positioning, defaults, or data
- old workflows do not disappear; they become training data or bottlenecks

Voice: more conversational if writing a short commentary. It can use first person lightly.

Structure:
1. start with a timely incident or two linked stories
2. dismiss the lazy take
3. identify the real incentive
4. show who pays for scaling
5. end with the next constraint

## Finance, fintech, and credit

Default lens: opacity, risk transfer, regulation, distribution, and consumer asymmetry.

Common angles:
- consumers experience a system no one can explain to them
- banks and fintechs benefit from opacity even if they complain about it
- legacy processes become moats when converted into data
- financial engineering creates headline profits but not necessarily operating strength

Structure:
1. open with a borrower, executive, call centre, or headline financial number
2. explain the official system
3. reveal the black box or hidden incentive
4. show why institutions tolerate it
5. end with the asymmetry unresolved

## Startups, VC, and founder stories

Default lens: narrative versus execution. Treat founder mythology as useful but insufficient.

Common angles:
- growth story versus unit economics
- ESOP wealth versus long hours
- IPO readiness versus operational mess
- family offices cutting out VC middlemen
- category creation versus cash-flow discipline

Structure:
1. open with a pitch, quote, or founder move
2. state the promise
3. compare promise with operating reality
4. explain the financing/incentive structure
5. reveal who absorbs the risk

## Consumer, FMCG, and food

Default lens: distribution, working capital, product mix, margins, and regional expansion.

Common angles:
- avoiding the obvious mass product can create better margins
- value-added products increase margin but complicate distribution
- brand love means little without cold chain, shelf space, and credit cycles
- premium positioning runs into habit, price, and regional incumbents

Structure:
1. open with factory/store/supply-chain detail
2. show why conventional wisdom points elsewhere
3. explain the company's counterintuitive choice
4. quantify revenue, margins, geography, and distribution limits
5. end with the expansion challenge

## Infrastructure and platform power

Default lens: control of chokepoints. Airports, marketplaces, logistics platforms, app stores, and payment rails should be treated as gatekeeping systems.

Common angles:
- operator moves from landlord to competitor
- partner ecosystem gets squeezed after creating the market
- vertical integration is rational for the platform and existential for intermediaries
- every square foot, transaction, or customer interaction becomes monetisable

Structure:
1. open with a seemingly routine announcement
2. reveal the target or displaced intermediary
3. explain the old handshake between players
4. show how the platform rewrites the equation
5. end with the new gatekeeper logic

## Education and policy

Default lens: institutional capacity, incentives, and student consequences. Avoid treating reform as implementation by announcement.

Common angles:
- moving online does not solve governance weakness
- benchmarking without participation creates performative reform
- private education products sell aspiration while shifting risk to students
- credential value depends on who grants the degree, not who markets the course

Structure:
1. open with student/exam/security/reform detail
2. show the stated problem
3. reveal the structural weakness
4. follow the burden onto students, states, vendors, or institutions
5. end with the gap between policy promise and capacity

## Mobility and marketplaces

Default lens: marketplace balance, driver economics, price-sensitive demand, and incumbent drift.

Common angles:
- a third player grows because both sides of the marketplace are frustrated
- subscription model versus commission model changes driver incentives
- expansion into higher-ticket categories changes the company's economics
- incumbents copy the disruptor once their moat weakens

Structure:
1. open with market-share shift or customer/driver pain
2. explain why incumbents created the opening
3. show the challenger mechanic
4. quantify market and unit-economics upside
5. end with the retaliation or scaling risk

## Telecom and regulated sectors

Default lens: survival as policy outcome. In regulated sectors, companies may live because the system needs them alive.

Common angles:
- accounting gains are mistaken for operating turnaround
- government support is framed as survival, not rescue
- strategic usefulness replaces normal competitive logic
- debt, dues, spectrum, banks, and promoters create a slow-motion equilibrium

Structure:
1. open with a surprising profit, stock move, or regulatory shift
2. revisit the earlier consensus
3. explain what rule changed
4. contrast financial optics with operating reality
5. end with survival as the business model
