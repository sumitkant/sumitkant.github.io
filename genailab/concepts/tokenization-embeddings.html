Tokenization and Embedding Models

Modern language systems begin by turning text into discrete symbols and then into continuous geometry. Tokenization decides what the model can see. Embeddings decide what the system can compare, retrieve, cluster, classify, and reason over. This guide connects both halves into one practical mental model.

First principles

The quiet contract between text and models

A language model does not receive words in the human sense. It receives token IDs: integers selected from a model-specific vocabulary. Those IDs are then looked up in an embedding table or passed through an embedding model, producing dense vectors. From there, attention layers, retrieval indexes, classifiers, clustering algorithms, and ranking systems operate on numbers.

That means tokenization and embeddings are not housekeeping details. They define the system's representational budget. A tokenizer decides whether unbelievable is one symbol, three subwords, or twelve characters. An embedding model decides whether two pieces of text land near each other because they share exact wording, semantic intent, domain context, language, or some mix of those signals.

The key distinction: tokenization is a compression and segmentation problem; embeddings are a representation and similarity problem. They are connected, but they optimize for different things.
1. NormalizeApply casing, Unicode, whitespace, or model-specific text rules.
2. TokenizeSplit text into vocabulary units and map each unit to an ID.
3. EmbedConvert IDs, words, sentences, chunks, images, or other objects into vectors.
4. CompareUse cosine similarity, dot product, distance, or a learned ranker.
5. ActRetrieve, classify, cluster, recommend, route, deduplicate, or ground generation.
Tokenization

Tokenization types, from simple splits to subword systems

Tokenization is the process of converting raw text into a sequence of units from a finite vocabulary. Early NLP systems often used words. Modern neural models usually use subwords, bytes, or sentencepiece-style units because they handle rare words, misspellings, morphology, code, and multilingual text more gracefully.

Word-level

Splits on words or linguistic boundaries. Easy to interpret, but vocabulary grows quickly and unknown words become a problem.

Readable

Character-level

Uses characters as tokens. Excellent coverage, but sequences become long and the model must learn larger semantic units from scratch.

High coverage

Byte-level

Starts from bytes, so any text can be represented. Byte-level BPE is common in GPT-style tokenizers and avoids unknown Unicode characters.

Robust

Byte Pair Encoding

Begins with small units and repeatedly merges frequent adjacent pairs until a target vocabulary size is reached.

Frequent merges

WordPiece

Popularized by BERT-style models. Similar in spirit to BPE, but merge choices are based on a likelihood-style objective rather than raw pair frequency.

BERT lineage

Unigram / SentencePiece

Starts with many candidate subwords, then prunes toward a compact vocabulary. SentencePiece can train directly from raw sentences.

Language-flexible

Why subwords became the default

Word-level tokenizers are intuitive but brittle. A production model cannot afford to treat every rare chemical name, new product, typo, username, URL, or agglutinative word as unknown. Character-level tokenization solves coverage but increases sequence length, which can make attention and training more expensive. Subword tokenization is the compromise: keep frequent words whole, split rare words into reusable fragments, and preserve open-vocabulary behavior.

Tokenizer family Core idea Strengths Tradeoffs Common examples
Word-level Vocabulary item per word or normalized word form. Human-readable, simple, useful for classic pipelines. Large vocabularies, poor rare-word handling, language-specific preprocessing. Classic bag-of-words, TF-IDF, older NLP systems.
Character-level Each Unicode character, grapheme, or character-like unit becomes a token. Excellent spelling and OOV coverage. Long sequences; semantics emerge over many steps. Some text generation, spelling-aware, low-resource experiments.
Byte-level BPE Start with 256 byte values, then learn frequent merges. Can represent arbitrary text without unknown Unicode tokens. Token counts may vary strongly across scripts and formatting. GPT-2-style tokenization; OpenAI's tiktoken is a fast BPE tokenizer.
BPE Iteratively merge frequent neighboring symbols. Efficient, deterministic, good rare-word handling. Frequency can favor dominant languages or domains in training data. GPT-family and RoBERTa-style tokenizers.
WordPiece Build subword vocabulary with a likelihood-inspired merge criterion. Strong fit for masked-language-model encoders. Usually tied tightly to the model that was pretrained with it. BERT and many BERT-derived encoders.
Unigram / SentencePiece Train a probabilistic inventory of subwords and select likely segmentations. Works directly on raw text; strong for multilingual settings. Less transparent than simple merge rules. T5, ALBERT, XLNet-style ecosystems; many multilingual models.

A small BPE story

Imagine a training corpus where token, tokens, tokenize, and tokenization appear frequently. BPE-like algorithms begin with small symbols, then merge frequent adjacent units. Over time, useful fragments such as token, ization, or ing become reusable. Rare words can still be represented because the tokenizer can fall back to smaller pieces.

Production rule: do not casually swap the tokenizer used by a pretrained model. Token IDs are part of the model's learned interface. Changing them is closer to rewiring the input layer than changing a text preprocessor.
Embedding models

Embeddings turn symbols, text, and objects into useful geometry

An embedding is a dense numerical representation. In older NLP, embeddings usually meant word vectors. In modern systems, embeddings may represent tokens inside a Transformer, whole sentences, document chunks, images, audio, users, products, queries, or multimodal objects. Similar items should land near one another in vector space.

Internal model embeddings

These are learned lookup vectors for token IDs inside a model. In Transformers, token embeddings are combined with positional information so the model can distinguish dog bites man from man bites dog.

Standalone embedding models

These produce vectors for external tasks: semantic search, clustering, recommendations, deduplication, classification, RAG retrieval, and anomaly detection. They are often consumed by vector databases or ranking pipelines.

Embedding type What it represents How it is learned Best used for Watch out for
One-hot vectors Discrete vocabulary identity. No learning; one dimension per vocabulary item. Teaching, simple linear baselines. Huge, sparse, no semantic similarity.
Word2Vec Words or phrases. Predicts context from word or word from context using shallow neural models. Classic semantic similarity, analogy exploration, lightweight features. Static meaning: one vector for bank regardless of sentence context.
GloVe Words. Factorizes global word co-occurrence statistics with a log-bilinear objective. Interpretable classic word vectors and quick baselines. Still static; vocabulary and corpus age matter.
Contextual token embeddings Token in a specific sentence. Transformer layers condition each token on surrounding tokens. NER, extraction, classification, fine-tuning, language understanding. Pooling token vectors into sentence vectors is not always optimal.
Sentence / text embeddings Sentence, query, paragraph, or document chunk. Often contrastive, siamese, dual-encoder, instruction-tuned, or task-tuned training. Semantic search, RAG, clustering, routing, recommendations. Must be evaluated on your task and domain; no universal winner.
Multimodal embeddings Text, images, audio, video, or mixed objects. Aligns different modalities into a shared space. Image search, product matching, media retrieval, cross-modal discovery. Similarity may reflect training data shortcuts or modality imbalance.

Similarity is not meaning; it is a trained behavior

In a search system, an embedding model may be trained so that a query and a relevant passage are close even if they use different words. In a classification system, the same text might be embedded so that class boundaries are easy to learn. In a recommendation system, similarity may reflect user behavior rather than semantic equivalence. Always ask: close according to what training signal?

Compare and contrast

How tokenization and embeddings influence each other

Tokenization and embeddings meet at the vocabulary. A tokenizer maps text into IDs; the model learns vector representations for those IDs. But the highest-leverage choices happen at different layers. Tokenizer decisions affect length, coverage, multilingual fairness, and out-of-vocabulary behavior. Embedding-model decisions affect retrieval quality, clustering shape, domain transfer, dimensionality, latency, and storage.

Question Tokenizer lens Embedding lens Practical decision
Will rare terms survive? Subword and byte tokenizers can decompose rare terms instead of dropping them. The embedding model still needs training signal for the domain meaning. For technical domains, test retrieval on real acronyms, product IDs, citations, and code names.
Will costs stay under control? More tokens mean more cost and shorter effective context. Higher dimensions improve capacity but increase storage and index memory. Measure tokens per document and bytes per vector before choosing chunk size and model size.
Will multilingual text work? Token length can vary across scripts and languages. Cross-lingual alignment depends on model training, not tokenization alone. Evaluate by language; do not assume English benchmark wins transfer cleanly.
Will exact terms matter? Tokenizers preserve surface form differently, especially punctuation, case, and whitespace. Dense embeddings may blur exact lexical matches. Use hybrid search when exact terms, part numbers, laws, or proper nouns are crucial.
Can I change it later? Changing tokenizer requires retraining or careful compatibility handling. Changing embedding model requires re-embedding your corpus and rebuilding indexes. Version tokenizers, embedding models, dimensions, chunking, and index settings together.

Production guidance

Best practices for reliable tokenization and embedding systems

Tokenizer best practices

  1. Use the tokenizer that belongs to the model. For pretrained models, tokenization is part of the contract. Use the official tokenizer package or the model provider's documented tokenizer.
  2. Measure tokens on your real corpus. Count tokens for representative documents, languages, code snippets, tables, URLs, and noisy user text before setting chunk sizes.
  3. Preserve important structure. Markdown headings, table rows, code blocks, legal sections, and citations often carry retrieval value. Normalize carefully.
  4. Watch multilingual token inflation. Some scripts, mixed-language text, and underrepresented languages may require more tokens for the same semantic content.
  5. Version the tokenizer and vocabulary. Store tokenizer name, version, vocabulary hash if available, and preprocessing rules alongside model artifacts.

Embedding best practices

  1. Pick the model for the task, not the leaderboard alone. MTEB is useful because it spans many tasks, but its own findings show that no one method dominates everything.
  2. Evaluate with your own queries and judgments. Build a small gold set: query, relevant documents, hard negatives, language/domain tags, and expected ranking.
  3. Choose chunking based on answerability. Chunks should be large enough to contain a coherent answer, but small enough to avoid semantic dilution.
  4. Use hybrid retrieval when wording matters. Dense vectors are strong for meaning; keyword indexes are strong for exact terms. Together they often beat either alone.
  5. Separate retrieval from final ranking. A fast embedding search can retrieve candidates; a cross-encoder, LLM reranker, or metadata filter can make the final choice.
  6. Normalize and monitor vectors. Know whether your similarity metric expects normalized vectors. Track embedding drift, empty inputs, duplicates, and outlier norms.
  7. Re-embed intentionally. Changing embedding model, dimensions, preprocessing, or chunking creates a new vector space. Rebuild indexes and compare before migration.
A practical default for RAG: use the provider's official tokenizer for counting, split documents by semantic structure, embed chunks with a model evaluated on your domain, retrieve with dense plus keyword search where exact terms matter, then rerank before generation.

Authoritative reference for SentencePiece, including its language-independent design and support for BPE and unigram language-model tokenization.

Closing synthesis

The engineering takeaway

Tokenizers make language computable by choosing the units. Embedding models make language useful by arranging those units, sentences, documents, and objects in a space where distance can drive product behavior. A strong system treats both as measurable infrastructure: count tokens, inspect segmentation, evaluate embeddings on real tasks, version every representation choice, and avoid assuming that a model trained for one kind of similarity will behave correctly for another.

The best practical stance is humble and empirical. Start with trusted model/tokenizer pairs, build a small evaluation set that reflects your users, test across languages and domains, and keep enough metadata to reproduce the vector space later.
