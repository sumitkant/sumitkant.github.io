How Transformers Work

A ground-up technical walkthrough of the Transformer architecture — from tokenisation and embeddings, through self-attention and multi-head attention, to the full encoder-decoder pipeline and its variants.

"Attention is All You Need" · Google, 2017 ~20 min read
Background

Why Transformers?

Earlier sequence models like RNNs and LSTMs processed words one at a time — left to right — which meant they struggled to relate words that were far apart in a sentence. Even after scaling these models, a fundamental limitation remained: they could only look at previous words, not the full context of an entire sentence, paragraph, or document.

The Transformer architecture, introduced in the 2017 paper "Attention is All You Need" by Google and the University of Toronto, solved this by:

  • Parallel processing of all input tokens simultaneously — enabling much larger training datasets
  • Efficient scaling — GPU parallelism made larger models practical
  • Learning attention — the ability to understand the meaning of words relative to every other word in the input
💡
The core insight The power of the transformer lies in its ability to learn and understand the meaning of all words in a sentence by learning the mapping between every word and every other word simultaneously — rather than sequentially.
Architecture Overview

The Encoder–Decoder Structure

At a high level, the Transformer is split into two distinct parts that work in conjunction:

Figure 1 — High-level Transformer architecture: the encoder processes the input, the decoder generates the output conditioned on the encoder's representation.

The encoder and decoder are similar in structure but serve different roles. The encoder reads and compresses the input into a rich contextual representation. The decoder generates the output token by token, attending to both previously generated tokens and the encoder's representation.

Input Processing

From Text to Vectors

Step 1 — Tokenisation

Raw text cannot be fed directly into a neural network. A Tokenizer converts words (or sub-word pieces) into integer IDs that the model can process.

  • Can tokenize at the word level or sub-word level (e.g. "learning" → ["learn", "##ing"])
  • The same tokenizer must be used for both training and inference — switching tokenizers invalidates all learned weights

Figure 2 — "J'aime l'apprentissage automatique" tokenised to integer IDs [2345, 3425, 3853]

Step 2 — Word Embeddings

Each token ID is looked up in an Embedding Layer — a trainable matrix that maps each ID to a high-dimensional vector. In the original Transformer paper, this vector has 512 dimensions.

The intuition: vectors learn to encode the meaning and context of tokens. Words with similar meanings cluster together in vector space. The angle between vectors measures semantic distance.

Figure 3 — Words represented as vectors in embedding space. The angle between vectors measures semantic similarity. "book" and "student" are closer than "book" and "fire".

Step 3 — Positional Encoding

Because the Transformer processes all tokens in parallel (unlike RNNs which process sequentially), it has no inherent sense of word order. Positional Encoding adds position information directly to each embedding vector.

A fixed positional encoding vector P (same dimension as the token embedding) is added to each token embedding, so the combined vector carries both meaning and position.

PEpos, 2i  =  sin(pos / 100002i/d)
PEpos, 2i+1  =  cos(pos / 100002i/d)

Positional encoding formulas — alternating sine and cosine for even/odd dimensions

pos
pos
Numerical position of the word in the sequence (e.g., "Jane" at pos = 1)
i
i
Refers to different dimensions of the encoding vector
d
d
Total dimension of the embedding vector (512 in original paper)

Positional Embedding in practice If word embeddings have 4 dimensions, a Positional Embedding vector P⁽¹⁾ of the same size is created for the first word. P⁽¹⁾ is directly added to the token embedding x⁽¹⁾, so that each embedding is "coloured" with its position information.
Self-Attention

Self-Attention: The Core Mechanism

Self-attention is the core innovation of the Transformer. For every word in the input, it computes an attention-based representation that captures the relationships between that word and every other word in the sentence — simultaneously.

This lets the model understand, for example, that in "The teacher taught the student with a book", the word book is closely related to teacher and student.

Figure 4 — Self-attention map: every word attends to every other word. The orange highlighted paths show the strongest attention weights — “book” attending strongly to “teacher” and “student”.

Query, Key, Value (Q, K, V)

For each word in the input, the model computes three vectors by multiplying the word embedding by three learned weight matrices WQ, WK, WV:

q
Query
The question this word is asking — "What is relevant to me?" Represents what the current word is looking for.
k
Key
The label each word offers — "Here's what I contain." Similarity of query·key determines attention weight.
v
Value
The actual content to pass forward — "Here's what I contribute." Weighted sum of values forms the output.

Worked Example: "Jan visite l'Afrique en Septembre"

Let's compute the attention representation A⁽³⁾ for the word l'Afrique (position 3). First, compute Q, K, V for x⁽³⁾:

q⟨3⟩  =  Wq · x⟨3⟩
k⟨3⟩  =  WK · x⟨3⟩
v⟨3⟩  =  WV · x⟨3⟩

where Wq, WK, WV are learnable weight matrices

The query q⁽³⁾ represents the question: "What's happening in l'Afrique?"

We then compute the dot product of q⁽³⁾ with every key in the sentence:

  • q⁽³⁾ · k⁽¹⁾ — How good is Jan as an answer? (A person → somewhat relevant)
  • q⁽³⁾ · k⁽²⁾ — How good is visite as an answer? (An action → very relevant! Africa is being visited)
  • q⁽³⁾ · k⁽⁴⁾ — How good is en (in) as an answer?
  • q⁽³⁾ · k⁽⁵⁾ — How good is Septembre as an answer?
🔍
Intuition behind the representation The word l'Afrique ends up knowing it is the destination of a visit. This is not a fixed embedding — it's a contextually enriched representation. l'Afrique in a different sentence (e.g., "Africa is a continent") would produce a completely different A⁽³⁾.

The Attention Formula

The full attention computation, using all queries, keys, and values in matrix form:

ATTENTION(Q, K, V)  =  softmax( QKT / √dk ) · V

Scaled Dot-Product Attention — the version from "Attention is All You Need". The √dk scaling prevents the dot products from growing too large.

The √dk scaling factor is crucial — without it, dot products can grow very large in high dimensions, pushing the softmax into regions with tiny gradients (making learning slow). This variant is also called Scaled Dot-Product Attention.

Multi-Head Attention

Multi-Head Attention

Self-attention is run multiple times in parallel — each run with its own learned Q, K, V weight matrices. Each parallel run is called an attention head.

Figure 5 — Self-attention computation for token x<3> (l’Afrique). Each token produces q, k, v vectors. The query q<3> is dotted against all keys — the orange path (q<3>·k<2>, “visite”) scores highest, showing l’Afrique attending strongly to the verb. Weighted values sum at the + node to produce output A<3>.

🧠
What each head learns The heads are not told what to learn. Their weights are randomly initialised and, given sufficient training data, each head discovers a different linguistic aspect: named entities, verb–object relationships, coreference, syntax patterns, etc. The number of heads varies: 12–100 are common in modern models.

After all heads run in parallel, their outputs are concatenated and passed through a final linear projection, giving a single output tensor of the same dimension as the input.

Full Network

Full Transformer Architecture

The complete Transformer stacks encoder and decoder blocks, each containing multiple sub-layers. Here is the canonical architecture from "Attention is All You Need":

Figure 6 — Complete Transformer architecture (from “Attention is All You Need”). Left: Encoder with feed-forward and multi-head attention blocks. Right: Decoder with masked attention and cross-attention. Zoom panels show the Multi-Head Attention internals and the Scaled Dot-Product Attention computation.

Key Sub-Layers Explained

  • Add & Norm — A residual connection (input + sub-layer output) followed by Layer Normalisation. Similar to Batch Normalisation in CNNs, this helps speed up learning and stabilises training in deep networks.
  • Residual Connections — Borrowed from ResNets, these carry positional information through the deep stack without it being washed out by repeated transformations.
  • Feed-Forward Network — A position-wise fully connected network applied identically to each position. Helps the model identify complex patterns and interactions in the attention output.
  • Masked Multi-Head Attention — Used only during training in the decoder. The mask hides future tokens so the model can't "cheat" by looking ahead. At test time, tokens are generated one by one so no masking is needed.
  • Linear & Softmax Layer — The decoder's final layer projects the output vector to vocabulary size, then softmax converts logits to probabilities over the full vocabulary.
Worked Example

Machine Translation: French → English

The original Transformer objective was sequence-to-sequence machine translation. Here's the complete step-by-step walkthrough of translating "J'aime l'apprentissage automatique" → "I love machine learning":

1
Tokenise the French input

The French sentence is tokenised using the same tokenizer used during training: "J'aime" → 2345, "l'apprentissage" → 3425, "automatique" → 3853

2
Add token IDs to encoder input

Token IDs [2345, 3425, 3853] are fed to the encoder side of the network as input.

3
Embedding + Multi-Head Attention (Encoder)

Tokens pass through the embedding layer + positional encoding, then into the encoder's Multi-Head Attention block. All 6 encoder blocks run in sequence.

4
Encoder output: rich contextual representation

After 6 encoder blocks with feed-forward networks, the encoder output encodes the full structure and meaning of the input. This is the "memory" the decoder will query.

5
Insert encoder output into decoder cross-attention

The encoder's K and V matrices are passed into the cross-attention layer in each decoder block. This is how the decoder "reads" the source sentence.

6
Decoder starts with <SOS> token

A Start-of-Sequence <SOS> token is fed to the decoder. The masked self-attention prevents it from seeing future tokens.

7
Cross-Attention: decoder queries encoder memory

The decoder uses Q from its own state to query the encoder's K and V: “What from the French sentence is most relevant to what I'm currently generating?”

8
Linear + Softmax → first token

Output passes through feed-forward → linear layer → softmax, producing a probability distribution over the full vocabulary. The highest probability token is selected: "I" (token ID 297).

9
Feed output back in; repeat

"I" is fed back as input to the decoder. Now the decoder generates "love", then "machine", then "learning", then finally the <EOS> End-of-Sequence token.

10
Detokenise to words

Token IDs [297, 450, 901, 389] are converted back to words: "I love machine learning".

Figure 7 — Full sequence-to-sequence translation pipeline. French tokens [2345, 3425, 3853] flow through the encoder. The decoder generates English tokens [297, 450, 901, 389] autoregressively — each decoded as “I love machine learning”.

Model Variants

Transformer Variants

The encoder and decoder components can be used independently or together, giving rise to three major families of transformer models:

Encoder-Only
Encoder Models
BERT RoBERTa ALBERT
Best for understanding tasks where the full context is available. Input and output sequences have the same length. Used for classification, sentiment analysis, named entity recognition, extractive QA.
Encoder-Decoder
Seq2Seq Models
T5 BARD BART
Best for sequence-to-sequence tasks where input and output lengths differ. Machine translation, summarisation, question answering with generation. The classic Transformer design.
Decoder-Only
Generative Models
GPT-4 LLaMA BLOOM Jurassic
Most powerful for open-ended text generation. The dominant paradigm for modern LLMs. Trained with next-token prediction on massive corpora. No encoder — the decoder attends to its own previous outputs.
📌
Modern LLMs are almost exclusively decoder-only GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, LLaMA, and most frontier models are decoder-only Transformers. They predict the next token conditioned on all previous tokens — the same architecture extended to billions of parameters with instruction fine-tuning and RLHF.
Summary

The Complete Picture

The Transformer architecture revolutionised NLP by replacing sequential processing with parallel, attention-based computation. Every component serves a specific purpose in building rich contextual representations:

Tokenisation
Converts text to integer IDs. Must be consistent between training and inference.
Embeddings
512D trainable vectors that encode semantic meaning. Similar words cluster together.
Positional Encoding
Sine/cosine vectors added to embeddings to preserve word order in parallel processing.
Self-Attention
Each word queries, keys, and values every other word. Produces context-aware representations.
Multi-Head Attention
H parallel attention operations, each learning a different linguistic relationship.
Encoder–Decoder
Encoder builds understanding. Decoder generates output by cross-attending to encoder's K, V.
✦
"Attention is All You Need" The original paper title is literal — the entire expressive power of the Transformer comes from attention mechanisms. No convolutions, no recurrence. Just learned attention weights, applied in parallel, at scale.
